Active Recall & Spaced Repetition — Evidence and Practical Setup

Key idea: testing yourself (recall) + revisiting on a schedule (spacing) builds durable memory far better than re-reading.

  • Why recall: It strengthens retrieval pathways. Reading recognizes; recall reconstructs.
  • Why spacing: Slight forgetting triggers deeper reconsolidation during review.
  • How to start: Create a deck (paper or app). After learning, quiz yourself before checking notes.
Read more: scheduling examples
  • Day 0: learn → quick self-test.
  • Day 1: review 10–15 min; flag hard items.
  • Day 3 & 7: short quizzes; retire mastered items.
  • Day 14+: weekly maintenance quiz.

Tip: Use 30–45 min focus blocks with 5–10 min breaks. One deep session beats hours of shallow reading.

Build Your Personal Learning System

Keep it simple: collect → distill → practice → teach.

  • Collect One inbox for ideas (notes app or paper).
  • Distill Daily 10-minute summaries: headings + bullets.
  • Practice Spaced repetition + weekly problem sets.
  • Teach Explain concepts to a friend or write a short post.

Research Reading Without Overwhelm

Skim for structure, then read for claims, then evaluate evidence. Take structured notes.

  1. Preview: abstract → headings → figures → conclusion.
  2. Questions: what is the claim? method? evidence?
  3. Notes: bullet insights + open questions; link to sources.
Read more: bias checklist
  • Is the sample representative?
  • Any confounders not controlled?
  • Do conclusions match the data?

4-Week Study Plan (General)

  1. Week 1: Foundations — key concepts, light recall, set up tools.
  2. Week 2: Practice — problem sets, mini-projects, feedback.
  3. Week 3: Integration — combine topics, teach someone else.
